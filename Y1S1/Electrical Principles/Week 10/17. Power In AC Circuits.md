## Calculations Of Power In Resistive Circuit

Power dissipated in load given by: 
$$P=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}$$
$$V_{\text{rms}}=I_{\text{rms}}R$$
$$P=I_{\text{rms}}^2R$$
$I_{\text{rms}}$ must be used - effective value of the current i.e. equivalent of a DC source (power-wise)

$$I_{\text{rms}}=\frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{R}$$
$$P=\frac{V_{\text{rms}}^2}{R}$$
$V_{\text{rms}}$ must be used - effective value of the voltage i.e. equivalent of a DC source (power-wise)

RMS values for sinusoidal waveforms
$$V_{\text{rms}}=\frac{V_{p}}{\sqrt{ 2 }}$$
$$I_{\text{rms}}=\frac{I_{p}}{\sqrt{2 }}$$

# Power In Reactive Circuits

Power in watts is called 'real' power
- Real power does work (i.e. creates movement, creates heat etc)
- Calculated using components of V & I that are in-phase with each other
Still use RMS values in AC circuits with reactance but:
- If phase difference exists between voltage & current, must resolve current and voltage onto common axis
![[Pasted image 20260112205040.png]]
$I_{\text{rms}}$ & $V_{\text{rms}}$ sometimes denotes the amplitude and phase of the quantity and sometimes only the amplitude; you may need to deduce which from context

There are other 'varieties' of power
- I.e. Reactive power and apparent power

## Real Power (Watts)
$$P=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\cos \theta\ \ (W)$$
## Reactive Power (Volts-Amps Reactive)
$$Q=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\sin \theta\ \ (VAR)$$
## Apparent Power (Volts-Amps)
$$S=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\ \ (VA)$$
## Power Factor
$$PF=\cos \theta=\frac{P}{S}$$
## Sign Of Reactive Power

![[Pasted image 20260112205419.png]]
$\theta$ defined, is phase angle of voltage measured with respect to current 
If voltage leads current (by up to $90^\circ$) then $\theta$ is +ve, $\sin \theta$ is +ve and Q is +ve
If voltage lags current (by up to $90^\circ$) then $\theta$ is -ve, $\sin \theta$ is -ve and Q is -ve

# Complex Apparent Power, Imaginary Reactive Power, Real Power

Real Power (P) measured in Watts (W)
Reactive Power (Q) measured in Volts Amps Reactive (VAR)
Complex Power (S) measured in $W+jVAR=P+jQ$
Apparent Power ($|S|=\sqrt{ P^2+Q^2 }$) measured in Volts Amps (VA)
Power Factor is $\cos \theta$

$$\begin{align*}
X_{C}&=\frac{1}{\omega C}=\frac{1}{314\times 100\times 10^{-6}}=31.85\ \Omega\\
Z&=R-jX_{C}=R-j \frac{1}{\omega C}=50-31.85j\ \Omega\\
V_{\text{rms}}&=\frac{V_{p}}{\sqrt{ 2 }}\\
V_{\text{rms}}\angle 0^\circ&=\frac{10}{\sqrt{ 2 }}\angle 0^\circ=7.071\angle 0^\circ\ \ (\text{rms})
\end{align*}$$
$$\begin{align*}
I_{p}&=\frac{V_{p}}{Z}=\frac{V_{p}}{R-j \frac{1}{\omega C}}\\
&=\frac{V_{p}}{R-j \frac{1}{\omega C}}\times \frac{R+j \frac{1}{\omega C}}{R+j \frac{1}{\omega C}}\\
&=\frac{V_{p}\left( R+j \frac{1}{\omega C} \right)}{R^2+\left( \frac{1}{\omega C} \right)^2}\\
&=\frac{10(50+31.85j)}{50^2+31.85^2}=0.1423+0.09063j\ A\\
&=\sqrt{ 0.1423^2 +0.09063^2}\angle \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{0.09063}{0.1423} \right)\\
&=0.1687\angle \tan^{-1}(0.6369)\\
I_{\text{rms}}&=0.1193\angle 32.5^\circ
\end{align*}$$

P&=7.071\times 0.1193\cos(-32.5^\circ)=0.711\ W\ \ (\text{Real Power})\\
Q&=7.071\times 0.1193\sin(-32.5^\circ)=-0.453\ VAR\ \ (\text{Reactive Power})\\
|S|&=7.071\times 0.1193=0.844\ VA\ \ (\text{Apparent Power})\\

\end{align*}$$

# Electricity Charges

Electricity companies charge for Apparent Power since:
- Transmission cables have to be large enough to carry current with acceptable volt-drop (irrespective of PF)
- $I^2R$ losses are proportional to current (irrespective of PF)
Apparent power is magnitude of (vector) sum of 
- Real power (that does work)
- Reactive power (that just flows back and forth between load and source)
This makes power factor important
- especially to industry

# Power Factor Correction

In an ideal world we want only real power 
- do not wish to pay for reactive power (which does no work) 
- hence power factor of $\cos \theta=1$ is desirable

We can compensate, or correct, the power factor by altering the overall impedance of the load

We add:
- Capacitance to compensate for PF created by a mostly inductive load (very common)
- Inductance to compensate for PF created by a mostly capacitive load (less common)

## Example 2

![[Pasted image 20260112212615.png]]
Determine $I_{\text{rms}}$, $\theta$, P, Q, S, R, L of motor

What practical capacitance would you add to improve PF?

Apparent power rating of motor $$|S|=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}=500\ VA$$
Therefore: 
$$\begin{align*}
I_{\text{rms}}&=\frac{|S|}{V}\\
I-\text{rms}&=\frac{500}{240}=2.083\ A

$$PF=\cos \theta=0.4\ (\text{lagging})$$
Therefore:
$$\theta=\cos^{-1}(0.4)= \pm 66.4^\circ$$
Choose + sign since PF is lagging (load is inductive)

![[Pasted image 20260112213032.png]]
$$\begin{align*}
P&=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\cos \theta=240\times 2.083\cos(66.4^\circ)\\
&=200.1\ W\ \ (\text{Real Power})\\
Q&=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\sin \theta=240\times 2.083\sin(66.4^\circ)\\
&=458.1\ VAR\  (\text{+ve since inductive load})\ (\text{Reactive Power})\\
|S|&=V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}=240\times 2.083\\
&=499.9\ VA\ (\text{this is what we pay for})\ \text{(Apparent Power)}
\end{align*}$$

![[Pasted image 20260112213330.png]]
$$\begin{align*}
Z&=\frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{I_{\text{rms}}}=\frac{240\angle 66.4^\circ}{2.083\angle 0^\circ}\\
&=115.2\angle 66.4^\circ\ \Omega
\end{align*}$$
So:
$$\begin{align*}
Z&=115.2\cos(66.4^\circ)+j 115.2\sin(66.4^\circ)\\
&=46.12+105.6j\\
Z&=R+jX_{L}\\
R&=46.12\ \Omega\\
X_{L}&=105.6\ \Omega\\
X_{L}&=2\pi fL\\
L&=\frac{X_{L}}{2\pi f}=\frac{105.6}{2\pi 50}\\
L&=0.336\ H =336\ mH
\end{align*}$$
Uncorrected reactive power is $$Q=458.1\ VAR\ \ (\text{inductive})$$
Reactive power of capacitor therefore needs to be: $$Q=-458.1\ VAR\ \ (\text{capacitive})$$
$$-Q=\frac{V^2}{X_{C}}$$
Therefore
$$X_{C}=\frac{V^2}{Q}=\frac{240^2}{458.1}=125.7\ \Omega$$
$$X_{C}=\frac{1}{2\pi fC}$$
Therefore
$$C=\frac{1}{2\pi fX_{C}}=\frac{1}{2\pi 50\times 125.7}=2.532\times 10^{-5}$$
Thus PF is corrected by installing a capacitor of $25.4\ \mu F$ in parallel with the motor

# Apparent Power In Ideal Transformers

![[Pasted image 20260112214312.png]]
If $\theta_{p}$ and $\theta_{s}$ are the angles between voltage and current in the primary and secondary windings respectively, power supplied to transformer by primary circuit is: $$P_{in}=V_{\text{rms,P}}I_{\text{rms,P}}\cos \theta_{P}$$
Power supplied to outer circuits: 
$$P_{out}=V_{\text{rms,S}}I_{\text{rms,S}}\cos \theta_{S}$$
Since there is no phase shift between primary and secondary windings of a (correctly connected) transformer, i.e.: $$\theta_{S}=\theta_{P}$$
Since (real) power out equals (real) power in (conservation of energy) then:
$$V_{\text{rms,S}}I_{\text{rms,S}}=V_{\text{rms,P}}I_{\text{rms,P}}$$
Thus apparent power is conserved across primary and secondary windings
