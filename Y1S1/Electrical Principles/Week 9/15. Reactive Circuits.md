# Reactance As 'Imaginary Resistance'

$$Z=R+j(X_{L}-X_{C})$$
Resistance R
Inductive reactance is positive imaginary 'resistance'
Capacitive reactance is negative imaginary 'resistance' 
Impedance of series elements is $Z=R+j(X_{L}-X_{C})$
Admittance of parallel elements is $Y=G+j(B_{C}-B_{L})$
Impedance is the reciprocal of admittance $Z=\frac{1}{Y}$

All rules for combining resistances and reactances are the same as for resistances alone (e.g. product over sum for parallel elements) but remembering the reactances are imaginary

# Susceptance As 'Imaginary Conductance'

$$Y=G+j(B_{C}-B_{L})$$
Conductance G
Capacitive susceptance is positive imaginary 'conductance'
Inductive susceptance is negative imaginary 'conductance' 
Admittance of parallel elements is $Y=G+j(B_{C}-B_{L})$
Impedance of series elements is $Z=R+j(X_{L}-X_{C})$
Admittance is the reciprocal of resistance $Y=\frac{1}{Z}$

All the rules combining conductances and susceptances are the same as for conductances alone (e.g. sum for parallel elements) but remembering the susceptances are imaginary 

# Capacitors In Parallel - Proof

![[Pasted image 20260112012106.png]]
Capacitance is proportional to capacitor plate area

If we connect capacitors in parallel we effectively make a capacitor with a plate area which is the sum of the individual capacitor plate areas

Total capacitance, $C_{T}$, is therefore sum of individual capacitors $$C_{T}=C_{1}+C_{2}+\dots+C_{N}$$
Since susceptance:
$$B_{C}=\omega C$$
$$B_{C,T}=B_{C_{1}}+B_{C_{2}}+\dots+B_{C_{N}}$$
Since reactance:
$$B_{C}=\frac{1}{X_{C}}$$
$$\frac{1}{X_{C,T}}=\frac{1}{X_{C_{1}}}+\frac{1}{X_{C_{2}}}+\dots+\frac{1}{X_{C_{N}}}$$
# Series Elements

Series resistances, reactances (and general impedances) add
This is intuitive since two elements in series make it harder for current to flow than each element alone
![[Pasted image 20260112012339.png]]
$$\begin{align*}
Z&=R_{1}+j\omega L_{1}+R_{2}+\frac{1}{j\omega C}+j\omega L_{2}\\
&=R_{1}+jX_{L_{1}}+R_{2}-jX_{C}+jX_{L_{2}}\ \ \Omega \\
\frac{1}{Y}&=\frac{1}{G_{1}}+\frac{j_{1}}{B_{L_{1}}}+\frac{1}{G_{2}}-j \frac{1}{B_{C}}+j \frac{1}{B_{L_{2}}}\ \ \Omega
\end{align*}$$

# Parallel Elements

Parallel conductances, susceptances (and general admittances) add 
This is intuitive since two elements in parallel make it easier for current to flow than each element alone
![[Pasted image 20260112012642.png]]
$$\begin{align*}
Y&=G_{1}+\frac{1}{j\omega L_{1}}+G_{2}+j\omega C+\frac{1}{j\omega L_{2}}\\
&=G_{1}-jB_{L_{1}}+G_{2}+jB_{C}-jB_{L_{2}}\ \ S\\
\frac{1}{Z}&=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{j\omega L_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}+j\omega C+\frac{1}{j\omega L_{2}}\ \ S
\end{align*}$$
# Mixed Series-Parallel Combinations

All the normal DC circuit analysis techniques can be used
E.g. Total impedance, Z, of the circuit opposite is given by: 
![[Pasted image 20260112012902.png]]
$$\frac{1}{Z}=\frac{1}{-jX_{C}}+\frac{1}{R+jX_{L}}$$
$$\frac{1}{Z}=\frac{1}{-j\left( \frac{1}{\omega C} \right)}+\frac{1}{R+j\omega L}$$
or: 
$$\frac{1}{Z}=\frac{1}{\left( \frac{1}{j\omega C} \right)}+\frac{1}{R+j\omega L}$$
Or use product over sum formula (for two parallel legs)
$$\Large Z=\frac{\frac{1}{j\omega C}(R+j\omega L)}{\frac{1}{j\omega C}+(R+j\omega L)}$$

# Choice Of Reference Phasor

Any phasor quantity (voltage across, or current through, any element) can be used as reference but...

For circuits that are purely series connected
- Current usually used as reference phasor
- Since same current flows through all (series connected) elements

For circuits that include parallel connections 
- Supply voltage usually used as reference phasor
- Since same voltage applied across all parallel connections

# Transient Response Of A Circuit

Until now we have assumed a circuit is driven by a sinusoid that has been present for a long (infinite) time

We can also drive circuits with signals that move from one steady state (e.g. 0 V) to a different steady state (e.g. 1 V)
- Signal changing (rapidly) from 0 V (DC) to any non-zero (DC) value is called a step function
- The resulting currents and voltages in the circuit are referred to as a transient response

# Step Response Of RC Circuit

![[Pasted image 20260112015843.png]]

Assume switch S is open and C is fully discharged 
For $t\leq 0,\ i=0,\ q=0,\ v_{C}=0$
S closes at $t=0$
$i$ begins to flow
C starts to charge 

Initially capacitor appears to be short circuit and maximum current flows restricted only by R

Initial current (at $t=0+$) is $$i(0)=\frac{V_{S}}{R}$$
Rate of increase in $q$ and $v_{C}$
- Both initially increase rapidly since no resisting charge/voltage
- Both decreases as $q$ and therefore $v_C$ build decreasing current

## Formal Statement Of Problem & Solution

Applying KCL to RC junction 
$$i=i_{C}$$
Expressing this in terms of voltages $$\frac{V_{S}-v_{C}}{R}=-C \frac{dv_{C}}{dt}$$
Rearranging to form standard (inhomogeneous) differential equation $$C \frac{dv_{C}}{dt}-\frac{1}{R} v_{C}+\frac{V_{S}}{R}=0$$
Solution (subject to initial condition $v_C(0)=0$) is: $$\Large v_{C}(t)=V_{S}(1-e^{-t/RC})$$
## Time Constant 

$$\tau=RC$$
$$\Large v_{C}(t)=V_{S}(1-e^{-t/RC})$$
![[Pasted image 20260112020359.png]]
Time taken for $v_{C}$ to reach fraction $1-e^{-1}=0.63$ of final value $V_{S}$ referred to as time constant 

At $t=\infty$ 
- $5\tau$ in practice
- $i=0,\ q=CV_{S}$

![[Pasted image 20260112020457.png]]
Proof:
$$\Large \begin{align*}
i(t)&=i_{C}(t)=-C \frac{dv_{C}}{dt}\\
&=-C \frac{d}{dt}[V_{S}(1-e^{-t/\tau})]\\
&=-C \frac{d}{dt}[V_{S}-V_{S}e^{-t/\tau}]\\
&=CV_{S} \frac{1}{\tau}e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}=CV_{S} \frac{1}{RC}e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}\\
&=\frac{V_{S}}{R}e^{\frac{t}{\tau}}
\end{align*}$$

# Step Response Of RL Circuit

![[Pasted image 20260112021244.png]]
Assume no current flowing, S open
For $t\leq 0,\ v_{L}(t)=0,\ i(t)=0$
S closes at $t=0$

Initially inductor appears to be open circuit and no current flows
Initial inductor voltage is $$v(0+)=V_{S}$$
KVL:
$$V_{S}=Ri+L \frac{di}{dt}$$
Inhomogeneous differential equation

Rate of increase in $i$
- Initially $i$ increases rapidly since entire voltage applied to inductor 
- Rate of increase in $i$ decreases as $i$ increases since increasing fraction of voltage dropped across R

## Time Constant

Solution of inhomogeneous differential equation:
$$\Large i(t)=\frac{V_{S}}{R}(1-e^{-\frac{R}{L}t})$$
![[Pasted image 20260112021530.png]]

$v_{L}(t)$ falls to $\frac{1}{e}=0.37$$ of initial value and $i_{L}(t)$ falls to $1-e^{-1}=0.63$ of initial value in $\tau$ seconds 

At $t=\infty$
- $5\tau$ in practice
- $i(t)=\frac{V_{S}}{R}$
- $v_L(t)=0$
