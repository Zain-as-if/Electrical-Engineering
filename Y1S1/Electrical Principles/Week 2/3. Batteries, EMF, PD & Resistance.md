DC - Direct Current
'Direct' means constant or unchanging (loosely)

AC - Alternating Current
Most common AC supply is the sinusoidal waveform 
AC is generated at the source (i.e. power plant) 
Number of completed cycles that are produced within one second (cycles/second) is called the Frequency (Hz)

Overall current in a cycle for AC is 0 
However regardless of direction, current still being delivered to a resistor in the circuit 
Therefore power still being delivered and it just varies with time

Frequency of an AC wave, $f=\frac{1}{T}$
T is the period of 1 full wave
Angular Frequency/Velocity is the rate of change of the phase of the sinusoidal wave $$\omega =2\pi f\ \ \text{rad/s}$$![[Pasted image 20260107030949.png]]
RMS (Root Mean Square) is the value that gives the same power dissipation in a resistive load (heating effect) as a numerically equal DC Value 

For a sinusoidal wave only: $$I_{RMS}=\frac{I_{pk}}{\sqrt{ 2 }}$$
The same can be applied for voltage where RMS is the value that gives the same power dissipation in a resistive load (heating effect) as a numerically equal DC Value

For a sinusoidal wave only: $$V_{RMS}=\frac{V_{pk}}{\sqrt{ 2 }}$$

# Batteries

DC **source** of electrical voltage, current and (therefore) energy
A **load** is something connected to the source that consumes electrical energy
Simplest model of a battery is a source with constant voltage 
- No voltage variation with time or with load
- Current drawn from battery depends on load (i.e. resistance in circuit)

A battery effectively places a (constant) +ve charge at one terminal & a (constant) -ve charge at the other
- As charge flows out of the battery terminals it is replaced by the chemical action of the battery
Electrons drift from -ve to +ve terminals through the external circuit ![[Pasted image 20260107031556.png]]
<h2>Battery Capacity + Factors</h2>
Capacity (Ampere-hour Rating) indicates how long a battery will supply a particular current at a rated voltage $$\text{Battery Life}=\frac{\text{Ampere-Hour (Ah) Rating}}{\text{Current Drawn}}$$
E.g. Battery with 200 Ah capacity will (theoretically) supply:
- 200 A for 1 Hour
- 100 A for 2 Hours
- 2 A for 100 Hours 

Actual capacity (Ah) of battery changes with:
- Current demand
- Temperature
- Age
- Number of charge/discharge cycles completed 
- Charge/discharge cycling pattern

E.g.
- Generally higher drain current the lower the actual Ah rating
- Maximum rated Ah usually given corresponding to room temperature

# Electromotive Force (Emf)
Capability of a battery (or any DC source) is called its electromotive force (emf)

Emf is measured in Volts (V)
- Emf is analogous to pressure in the hydrological analogy
- Emf often denoted by $V$ or $V_{emf}$

All voltage sources have 2 fundamental parts - Source of electrical energy that has a characteristic electromotive force (Emf) and an internal resistance $r$

Emf is the potential difference of a source when no current is flowing 

Numerical value of the emf depends on the source of potential difference

# Limits Of Current
Electrons have thermal energy (i.e. random motion) as well as average motion (drift velocity)

Thermal motion means they continually hit the copper ions which slow them down

This is origin of electrical resistance which is what limits the current flow

LEDs (Light Emitting Diodes) are semiconductor light sources that combine a P-type semiconductor (Larger hole concentration) with an N-type semiconductor (Larger electron concentration) 

Applying a sufficient forward voltage will cause the electrons and holes to recombine at the P-N junction releasing energy in the form of light

Electrostatic field is a force field - defined by force on a small +ve point charge divided by the size of the point charge $$E(x,y,z)=\frac{F_{\delta q}(x,y,z)}{q}\ \ [N/C]$$
Work (w) is done by moving a charge from a to b against the force (F=qE) exerted on the charge by an electric field (E)$$\text{Work (W)}=\text{Force (F)}\cdot \text{Distance Moved (L)}$$
$$W_{ba}=W_{b}-W_{a}=FL=qEL\ \ (J)$$
