Uses mutual inductance between two coils

Coil connected to AC source is called primary winding

Coil connected to load is called secondary winding

Varying current in primary winding creates varying magnetic flux

High-permeability (e.g. iron) core constrains flux generated by primary winding 'directing' it through secondary winding 

Varying flux indicates emf in secondary winding 

Laminations interrupt eddy currents and therefore reduce eddy current losses

## Transformer Voltage Relationship

Voltage magnitude in secondary winding depends on number of turns in primary $N_{p}$ and secondary $N_s$ windings

If primary winding voltage is sinusoidal then secondary winding voltage will be sinusoidal and RMS voltages are related by: $$V_{S}=-\frac{N_{S}}{N_{P}}V_{P}$$
-ve sign removed by reversing polarity of one winding 
Rearranging gives:
$$\frac{V_{S}}{V_{P}}=\frac{N_{S}}{N_{P}}$$
If $V_{S}<V_{P}$ transformer is step-down
If $V_{S}>V_{P}$ transformer is step-up

## Transformer Apparent Power Relationship

Apparent power ($V_{\text{rms}}\times I_{\text{rms}}$) is conserved in an ideal transformer $$V_{S}I_{S}=V_{P}I_{P}$$
This is very intuitive 
If transformer is ideal
- i.e. lossless
then power out = power in
- otherwise where would energy go to or come from

## Transformer Current Relationship

Rearranging the apparent power relationship
$$\frac{V_{S}}{V_{P}}=\frac{N_{S}}{N_{P}}$$
And substituting the voltage relationship it yields:
$$\frac{N_{S}}{N_{P}}=\frac{I_{P}}{I_{S}}$$

Combined Ideal transformer relationships:
$$\frac{V_{S}}{V_{P}}=\frac{I_{P}}{I_{S}}=\frac{N_{S}}{N_{P}}$$
## Phase Relationship

In an ideal transformer there is no phase shift between $V_{P}$ and $V_{S}$ and no phase shift between $I_{P}$ and $I_S$ 
- Providing coil terminals defined correctly
- If one pair of coil terminals reversed then phase shift is $180^\circ$

![[Pasted image 20260112215745.png]]
Since primary winding is an inductor, current in primary winding lags voltage applied across primary winding by $90^\circ$ 

Magnetic flux flowing through core is in phase with primary winding current so it also lags primary winding voltage by $90^\circ$

Voltage induced in secondary winding given by Faraday's law:
$$v_{s}=-N_{s} \frac{d\varphi}{dt}$$
Negative sign is $180^\circ$ phase shift from flux to induced voltage, $\frac{d}{dt}$ is $90^\circ$ advance from flux to induced voltage so total phase shift from flux to secondary voltage is $270^\circ$ advance or $90^\circ$ lag

Phase shift from primary voltage to secondary voltage is thus either $180^\circ$ or $0^\circ$ depending on secondary winding connection polarity

# Auto Transformers

Transformer with single winding
- Part of winding serves as both primary and secondary
- Usually step-down but can be step-up
Advantages (with respect to conventional dual winding)
- Smaller
- Lighter
- Cheaper
- Lower leakage loss
Disadvantages
- No galvanic (DC electrical) isolation between primary and secondary circuit

# Core & Shell Type Constructions

![[Pasted image 20260112220300.png]]
Core type - Windings surround core ring on its outer edges
- Half winding wrapped around each limb of the core
Shell type - Windings located inside core ring such that core forms a shell outside windings
- Prevents flux leakages since both windings are wrapped around the same centre limb

## EI (Shell-Type) Construction

![[Pasted image 20260112220347.png]]
Changing flux in conducting core creates eddy current circulating in plane perpendicular to magnetic flux 

Eddy currents results in $I^2R$ losses

Laminating core reduces eddy currents and therefore reduces eddy current losses

Inverting E and I sections on alternate lamination layers reduces high reluctance 'gap' that might otherwise occur

## Reduction Of Eddy Currents Using Laminated Core

$I^2R$ loss due to sum of many smaller eddy currents is less than loss due to one larger eddy current

# Use Of Transformers In A 3-Phase Transmission/Distribution System

![[Pasted image 20260112220959.png]]

Why step-up voltage for transmission and step-down voltages for use at the load?
- Why not generate and transmit energy at voltage it is generated (or even lower for safer voltage)?
- We could always step up voltage if needed at load

Because at higher voltages we need thinner (and therefore lighter and cheaper) cables to transmit a given amount of power 
- Ohmic losses of conductors ($I^2R$) are much greater than dielectric losses of insulation $\frac{V^2}{R}$

# Instrument Transformers

Dividing into two types
- Current Transformers (CTs)
- Potential Transformers (PTs)
	- Also called voltage transformers (VTs)

CTs sample current in transmission line or bus-bar and reduce it to safe, measurable level
PTs (or VTs) sample voltage and reduce it to safe, measurable level

## Potential Transformers

High-voltage primary, low-voltage secondary, very low power rating

Used to provide accurate voltage samples to instruments monitoring voltage in power system

Also called Voltage Transformers (VTs)

Not different in any essential way to other transformer
![[Pasted image 20260112221346.png]]

## Current Transformer

![[Pasted image 20260112221524.png]]
Consists of secondary winding wound on ferromagnetic core and (single-turn) primary winding comprising transmission line passing through core

Small sample of flux circulating around transmission line concentrated in ferrite ring 

Changing flux in ferrite ring induces voltage in secondary winding 

Fact it is a sample of the primary flux means CTs suffer large flux leakage and the ideal transformer equation (implying conservation of power) doesn't apply

Windings in current transformers are loosely coupled 
- Mutual flux is smaller than leakage flux 

CTs must be 'short-circuited' at all times when primary circuit energized since very high voltages will appear across CTs open-circuited terminals
