Capacitance is the ability (capacity) to store charge
Capacitor is a charge (and electric field) storage device

Commonly a pair of conducting plates separated by insulating dielectric 

Capacitance defined by: $$C=\frac{Q}{V}\ [C/V\ \text{or farad}\ (F)]$$
Therefore: $$Q=CV\ [C]$$
Typical range of conventional capacitor values:
$$1\ pF - 1\ mF$$
High permittivity dielectrics increase electric flux density for a given capacitor voltage and thus increases stored energy and therefore increases capacitance

# Capacitance 

$$C=\frac{\epsilon_{0}\epsilon_{r}A}{d}\ (F)$$
Where: 
$A$ = Plate Area ($m^2$)
$\epsilon_{0}$ = Permittivity Of Free Space ($F/m$)
$\epsilon_{r}$ = Relative Permittivity Of Dielectric 
$d$ = Plate Spacing ($m$)

![[Pasted image 20260111005029.png]]
$$D=\frac{Q}{A}\ (C/m^2)$$
## Derivation Of Capacitance Of Parallel-Plate Capacitor 

$$\begin{align*}
D&=\frac{Q}{A}\\
E&=\frac{D}{\epsilon}=\frac{Q}{\epsilon A}\\
V&=Ed=\frac{Q}{\epsilon A}d\\
C&=\frac{Q}{V}=\frac{\epsilon A}{d}=\frac{\epsilon_{0}\epsilon_{r}A}{d}\ (F)
\end{align*}$$

## Electrolytic Capacitors 

Large capacitance but polarised 
- One terminal must always be +ve with respect to the other

Anode electrode (+) is aluminium foil
Aluminium oxide forms thin dielectric layer

Electrolyte forms (-ve) electrode (but is in contact with second aluminium foil that acts as physical terminal)

Polarity must be respected otherwise capacitor may explode

Must take care not to overload capacitors 

Often used as smoothing filter in power supplies

# Capacitive Reactance

![[Pasted image 20260111010255.png]]

Since $$i(t)=C \frac{dv(t)}{dt}$$

For sinusoidal voltages we can replace $d()/dt$ with $\times j\omega$ $$i(t)=C\ j\omega\ v(t)$$
This is because for sinusoidal voltages differentiating a sinewave results in a cosine wave:
$$\frac{d}{dt}[\sin(\omega t)]=\omega \cos (\omega t)$$
Multiplying a phasor by $j$ rotates the phasor through $+90^\circ$ (or $+\frac{\pi}{2}$ rad) 
Sinewave is represented on a phasor diagram by an arrow pointing down ($-j$ lying along the negative $j$-axis)

Rotating the $-j$ phasor through $+90^\circ$ (anticlockwise) brings it to the +ve real axis
![[Pasted image 20260111012741.png]]

Or: $$v(t)=\frac{1}{j\omega C}i(t)$$

Remember: 
$$1/j=-j$$
$$V_{\text{rms}}=-jX_{c}I_{\text{rms}}$$
I.e. 
$$X_{c}=\frac{1}{(\omega C)}\ (\Omega)$$
Reactance is a 'resistance' to AC current flow

# Capacitive Susceptance

Susceptance is reciprocal of reactance $$B_{c}=\frac{1}{X_{c}}=\omega C$$
Therefore:
$$v(t)=\frac{1}{jB_{c}}i(t)=-j \frac{i(t)}{B_c}$$
Or: $$i(t)=jB_{c}v(t)$$
In terms of rms quantities $$I_{\text{rms}}=jB_{c}V_{\text{rms}}$$
Susceptance is the **ease** with which a voltage can force an AC current through a capacitor 

# Impedance & Admittance Quantities Compared

Impedance, Z, is the general term for either resistance or reactance or a combination of the two

Z includes the phase relationship between current and voltage so for a capacitor: $$V_{\text{rms}}=ZI_{\text{rms}}=-jX_{c}I_{\text{rms}}$$
Admittance, Y, is reciprocal of impedance

# Capacitors In Parallel

![[Pasted image 20260111011317.png]]
Capacitance is proportional to capacitor plate area

If we connect capacitors in parallel we effectively make a capacitor with a plate area which is the sum of the individual plate areas

Total capacitance, $C_{T}$ is therefore sum of individual capacitors $$C_{T}=C_{1}+C_{2}+\dots+C_{N}$$
Since capacitive susceptance is $B_{c}=\omega C$
$$B_{C_{T}}=B_{C_{1}}+B_{C_{2}}+\dots+B_{C_{N}}$$
Since capacitive reactance is $X_{c}=\frac{1}{B_{c}}$
$$\frac{1}{X_{C_{T}}}=\frac{1}{X_{C_{1}}}+\frac{1}{X_{C_{2}}}+\dots+\frac{1}{X_{C_{N}}}$$
Capacitance add when in parallel 
Capacitance susceptances add when in parallel
Reciprocal of capacitive reactances add when in parallel 

# Capacitors In Series

![[Pasted image 20260111011326.png]]
Assume $V_T$ across the series combination 

Charge $Q$ must be the same (magnitude) on each plate irrespective of capacitor value 
- Because charge on each plate comes from the plate of the capacitor connected to it 
$$V_{T}+V_{1}+V_{2}+\dots+V_{N}$$
Using $Q=CV$
$$\frac{Q}{C_{T}}=\frac{Q}{C_{1}}+\frac{Q}{C_{2}}+\dots+\frac{Q}{C_{N}}$$
Therefore: 
$$\frac{1}{C_{T}}=\frac{1}{C_{1}}+\frac{1}{C_{2}}+\dots+\frac{1}{C_{N}}$$
Dividing by $\omega$
$$\frac{1}{\omega C_{T}}=\frac{1}{\omega C_{1}}+\frac{1}{\omega C_{2}}+\dots+\frac{1}{\omega C_{N}}$$
Since $X_{c}=\frac{1}{\omega C}\ (\Omega)$
$$X_{C_{T}}=X_{C_{1}}+X_{C_{2}}+\dots+X_{C_{N}}$$
And Since $B_{C}=\frac{1}{X_{C}}$
$$\frac{1}{B_{C_{T}}}=\frac{1}{B_{C_{1}}}+\frac{1}{B_{C_{2}}}+\dots+\frac{1}{B_{C_{N}}}$$

Reciprocal of capacitances add when in series

Capacitive reactances add when in series

Reciprocal of capacitive susceptances add when in series

# Supercapacitors

Also referred to as ultracapacitors or Goldcaps

Energy storage devices

Capacitance as high as $12000\ F$

Use two mechanism to store electrical energy 
- Double layer capacitance - electrostatic in origin
- Pseudo-capacitance - electrochemical (like batteries)

Polar devices (like electrolytic capacitors)

Low maximum voltage (typically between $2.5-2.7\ V$)

Fast charge and discharge times

Can tolerate many more charge and discharge cycles than rechargeable batteries

Fills technology gap between electrolytic capacitors and rechargeable batteries

## Supercapacitor Operating Principles

No dielectric but space between electrodes filled with electrolyte

Electrodes made from metal coated with a porous substance (e.g. powdery, activated charcoal) which gives large area

When electrodes polarized by applied voltage, ions in electrolyte form electric double layers at surface of electrode (maybe just one molecule thick)
- Layer is thin resulting in large capacitance

Some ions permeate double layer becoming specifically absorbed ions and contribute to pseudo-capacitance

Separator is ion-permeable membrane insulator which might be made of carbon, paper, or plastic
- Separator keeps electrodes apart to prevent electrical short circuits whilst allowing transport of ion charge carriers needed to close circuit during the passage of current in  an electrochemical cell
