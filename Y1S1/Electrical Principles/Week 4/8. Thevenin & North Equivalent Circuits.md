# Thevenin's Theorem

Any two-terminal linear network can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a **single voltage source** and a **series resistor**

Allows part of a complex circuit to be reduced to a simple form allowing concentration on the rest

Note: Circuits are only equivalent at the terminals
- It supplies the same voltage, current and power as the original circuit to the load

![[Pasted image 20260109035853.png]]

## Technique

1. Remove the part of the circuit you don't want to reduce (usually the load)
2. Label the terminals, e.g. a and b
3. Short circuit Voltage sources and open circuit current sources and find resistance between the two terminals (This is $R_{\text{Th}}$- the resistance of the Thevenin Circuit)
4. Find the open circuit  voltage across terminals a and b (this is $E_{\text{Th}}$- the source voltage of the Thevenin Circuit)
5. Redraw the Thevenin equivalent circuit

### E.g. 1

Step 1:

Step 4:

Because a, b are O/C no current flows through $R_{2}$
$E_{\text{Th}}$ is thus voltage across $R_{1}$
$$E_{\text{Th}}=IR_{1}=12\times 4=48\ V$$

Step 1 & 2:

Find $R_{\text{Th}}$ between a and b
Since $R_{3}$ is S/C

\end{align*}$$

### E.g. 3

![[Pasted image 20260109041940.png]]
Find Thevenin equivalent circuit excluding the load $R_{L}$ and the current through $R_{L}$ for values $2\ k\Omega, 10\ k\Omega, 100\ k\Omega$

Step 1 & 2: 
Remove load and label terminals

Step 3: 
Find resistance between a and b
Set voltage sources to 0 V and S/C them (Behave likes normal wires, 0 resistance)
$$\begin{align*}
\frac{1}{R_{x}}&=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}+\frac{1}{R_{3}}\\
\frac{1}{R_{x}}&=\frac{1}{0.8}+\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{5}{3}\\
R_{x}&=0.6\ k\Omega\\
R_{\text{Th}}&=0.6+1.4=2\ k\Omega
\end{align*}$$
Step 4.1:
Find O/C voltage $E_{\text{Th},1}$ due to $E_{1}$ across a-b
As a-b is O/C there is no current flow through $R_{4}$
$\therefore$ no voltage drop across $R_{4}$ 
![[Pasted image 20260109042539.png]]
![[Pasted image 20260109042544.png]]
$$\begin{align*}
\frac{1}{R_{y}}&=\frac{1}{R_{2}}+\frac{1}{R_{3}}=\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{10}{24}\\
R_{y}&=2.4\ k\Omega\\
R_{T}&=2.4+0.8=3.2\ k\Omega\\
E_{\text{Th},1}&=E_{1} \frac{R_{y}}{R_{T}}=6\times \frac{2.4}{3.2}=4.5\ V\\
\end{align*}$$
Step 4.2:
Find O/C voltage $E_{\text{Th},2}$ due to $E_{1}$ across a-b 
As a-b is O/C there is no current flow through $R_{4}$
$\therefore$ no voltage drop across $R_{4}$
![[Pasted image 20260109043023.png]]
$$\begin{align*}
\frac{1}{Rz}&=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{3}}=\frac{1}{0.8}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{17}{12}\\
R_{z}&=\frac{12}{17}\ k\Omega\\
R_{T}&=\frac{12}{17}+4=\frac{80}{17}\ k\Omega\\
E_{\text{Th},2}&=E_{2} \frac{R_{z}}{R_{T}}=10\times \frac{\frac{12}{17}}{\frac{80}{17}}=1.5\ V
\end{align*}$$
Using superposition:
Since $E_{\text{Th},1}$ and $E_{\text{Th},2}$ have different polarities and taking $E_{\text{Th},1}$ as the positive polarity:
$$\begin{align*}
E_{\text{Th},1}&=4.5\ V, E_{\text{Th},2}=-1.5\ V\\
E_{\text{Th}}&=E_{\text{Th},1}+E_{\text{Th},2}\\
E_{\text{Th}}&=4.5-1.5=3\ V
\end{align*}$$

Redraw

# Norton's Theorem

Any two terminal linear circuit can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a **single current source** and **parallel resistor**

Allows part of a complex circuit to be reduced to a simple form allowing concentration on the rest

Note: Circuits are only equivalent at the terminals
- It supplies the same voltage, current and power as the original circuit to the load

![[Pasted image 20260109044431.png]]

## Technique 

1. Remove the part of circuit you don't want to reduce (usually the load)
2. Label terminals a and b
3. Short circuit voltage sources and open circuit current sources - find the parallel resistance $R_{N}$ following the path between the terminals at a and b (same procedure as for Thevenin's Theorem so $R_{N}$=$R_{\text{Th}}$) 
4. Replace all sources and determine S/C current $I_{N}$ through terminals a and b
5. Redraw Norton equivalent circuit

## Examples

### E.g. 1

![[Pasted image 20260109044914.png]]
Find Norton equivalent circuit for components apart from $R_{L}$

Step 1 & 2:
Remove part of circuit not being considered
Label terminals a and b
![[Pasted image 20260109044950.png]]

Step 3:
Find $R_{N}$ 'seen' through a and b
This means acting as if $E_{1}$ is S/C so resistors are branched
$$\begin{align*}
\frac{1}{R_{N}}&=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}=\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{3}{6}\\
R_{N}&=2\ \Omega
\end{align*}$$
Step 4: Find the S/C current $I_{N}$ through a-b
$I_{N}$ will take the zero-resistant way so $R_{2}$ ignored
$$I_{N}=\frac{E}{R_{1}}=\frac{9}{3}=3\ A$$
Step 5: 
Redraw

Step 1 & 2:

Step 3: 
Find $R_{N}$ 'seen' through a-b
$$R_{N}=4+5=9\ \Omega$$

Step 4:

$R_{1}$ ignored as $R_{1}>R_{2}$

\frac{1}{R_{T}}&=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}=\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{5}=\frac{9}{20}\\
R_{T}&=\frac{20}{9}\\
I_{N}&=I_{s} \frac{R_{T}}{R_{2}}=10\times \frac{\frac{20}{9}}{4}=\frac{50}{9}\ A
\end{align*}$$

### E.g. 3

![[Pasted image 20260109050231.png]]
Find Norton equivalent circuit for the components left of a-b

Step 1 & 2:
Redraw and label terminals a-b
![[Pasted image 20260109050254.png]]

Step 3:
Find $R_{N}$ 'seen' through terminals a-b
$$\begin{align*}
\frac{1}{R_{N}}&=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}\\
\frac{1}{R_{N}}&=\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{10}{24}\\
R_{N}&=2.4\ \Omega
\end{align*}$$
![[Pasted image 20260109050715.png]]
Step 4.1:
Find the S/C current $I_{N}'$ due to $E_{1}$ across a-b
$I_{N}'$ will take the zero-resistance path so $R_{2}$ has no effect
$$I_{N}'=\frac{E_{1}}{R_{1}}=\frac{7}{4}=1.75\ A$$
![[Pasted image 20260109050723.png]]
Step 4.2: 
Find the S/C current $I_{N}''$ due to $I$ across a-b 
$I_{N}''$ will take the zero-resistance path so $R_{2}$ and $R_{1}$ have no effect and all of $I$ flows from b to a
$$\therefore I_{N}''=I=8\ A$$
$I_{N}'$ and $I_{N}''$ are flowing in opposite directions, hence, the overall $I_{N}$ is:
$$I_{N}=(-I_{N}')+I_{N}''=-1.75+8=6.25\ A\ (b-a)$$

Redraw

# Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

What load should be applied to a system to ensure that load is receiving maximum power ?

$$P=I^2R$$
$$P=\frac{V^2}{R}$$
"The maximum amount of power will be dissipated in the load resistance if it is equal in value to the Thevenin or Norton source resistance of the networking supplying the power"

![[Pasted image 20260109051115.png]]
To find where $P_{L}$ is a maximum we differentiate the equation and set the result to 0

This means that we are finding the gradient and looking for the point where it is zero
- i.e. the peak maximum where the gradient goes from +ve to -ve
