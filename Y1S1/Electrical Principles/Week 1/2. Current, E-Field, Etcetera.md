# Current

Free electrons are the charge carriers in a conducting material. With enough thermal energy the valence electron becomes delocalised from the atom. W/ no external forces applied, the net flow of charge in any one direction is zero. 

If external +ve charge applied to one end of piece of conducting material, then electrons attracted to +ve charge. 

Current is the flow rate of charge ($Cs^-1$) 

Direction of electron flow 
-ve -> +ve 

Direction of current flow 
+ve -> -ve
![[Pasted image 20251124131458.png]]

However in semi-conductors the flow of charge can be either direction due to electron flow or a flow of +ve charge

Ampere - Constant current which when flowing in two infinitely long, straight thin parallel conductors spaced one metre apart in a vacuum produces a specific force of $2\cdot10^{-7}$ N/m between the conductors 

# Charge

Electrical charge is defined as the time integral of current 
$$q = \int I(t)\ dt\ \text{[A s or C]}$$ If current is constant
$$q=It$$
Unit of charge (Coulomb, C) is the charge passing a point when one ampere flows for one second
$$q_e=-1.602\cdot 10^{-19}$$
-1C is the charge of $6.242\cdot 10^{18}$ electrons
Hence if 1C flows through a cross section line of a conductor in one second, then there is one ampere (A) flowing in the conductor

# Electric Flux

Often imagine electric fields to derive from a fluid like substance which we call electric flux ($\psi$)
Electric flux shown with the electric flux lines - Denser lines the stronger the electric field![[Pasted image 20251230010255.png]]
Electric flux is a measure of the flow of electrical field lines through a given surface

Larger the charge on the plates, Q, the greater number of flux lines
So $\psi \equiv Q$ (Unit of flux is C)
# Surface Charge Density

Now assume each red dot is 1 $\mu C$ of charge, and plates are squares of side 1 cm
![[Pasted image 20251230010510.png]]
Surface charge density, $\sigma$, on +ve plate is: $$\sigma=\frac{q}{A}=\frac{4 \cdot (1 \cdot 10^{-6})}{(1\cdot{10^{-2})^2}}=0.04\ \text{C/m}^2$$
Where $\sigma$ = Surface charge density, q = total electric charge, A = area of surface
Surface charge density of -ve plate is $\sigma=-0.04\ \text{C/m}^2$
# Electric Flux Density

Total flux flowing between plates (using previous example) is: $$\psi=q=4\ \mu C$$
Amount of flux per square metre (measured perpendicular to flux flow) is called flux density, D $(\text{C/m}^2)$ ![[Pasted image 20251230011306.png]]
Magnitude of flux density between plates is: $$D=\sigma=\frac{q}{A}=\frac{\psi}{A}=\frac{4\cdot 10^{-6}}{(1\cdot 10^{-2})^2}=0.04\ \text{C/m}^2$$
Relationship between Density and Electric field strength: $$D=\epsilon E$$
$\epsilon$ is the permittivity of the material in which the field exists and is a property of the material 
When the field exists in free space (i.e. a vacuum) the permittivity is that of free space: $$\epsilon_{0}=8.854\cdot 10^{-12}\ \text{F/m}$$
Different materials between the plates of the capacitor can store additional charge (have to be insulators)

Relative permittivity ($\epsilon_{r}$) of a material is defined by: $$\epsilon_{r}=\frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_{0}}$$$$D=\epsilon_{r}\epsilon_{0}E$$
