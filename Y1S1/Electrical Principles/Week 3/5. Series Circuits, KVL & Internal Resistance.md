# Resistors 

The current is the same through series elements 
Total resistance in a series circuit is the sum of all the resistances 
$$R_{T}=R_{1}+R_{2}+R_{3}+\dots+R_{N}\ \text{For N Resistors In Series}$$
Source only "sees" the total resistance in a circuit 
Ohms law can be used to find the current $$I_{s}=\frac{E}{R_{T}}$$
Where E=EMF, i.e. The Supply Voltage
Since $E$ is fixed, $I_{s}$ depends only on $R_{T}$

# Power

Power delivered to each resistor can be found using: $$\begin{align*}
P_{N}&=V_{N}I\\
P_{N}&=I^2R_{N}\\
P_{N}&=\frac{V_{N}^2}{R_{N}}
\end{align*}$$
Power delivered by source is: $$P=EI$$
And $$P=P_{1}+P_{2}+\dots+P_{N}$$

# Kirchhoff's Voltage Law

[[6. Parallel Circuits, Conductance, KCL & Loading Effects#Kirchhoff's Current Law]]

Algebraic sum of potential rises and drops around a closed loop or path is zero
$$\sum V=0$$
![[Pasted image 20260108033026.png]]
1. Standardise on clockwise direction 
2. a-b voltage drops across $R_{1}\ (V_{1})$
3. b-c voltage drops across $R_{2}\ (V_{2})$
4. c-a voltage rises across voltage source 
5. KVL says that: 
$$E-V_{1}-V_{2}=0$$
Voltage drops are -ve
Voltage rises are +ve

Positive sign when moving from a negative to a positive potential (like moving from a negative bank balance to positive one)

Negative sign when moving from positive potential to negative one

-ve -> +ve = +
+ve -> -ve = -

E.g.
![[Pasted image 20260108033342.png]]
Determine $V_{s}$
$$\begin{align*}
20-V_{s}-12&=0\\
\therefore V_{s}=20-12&=8\ V
\end{align*}$$

# Voltage Divider Rule

Voltage across resistive elements will divide according to the resistances $$V_{x}=E \frac{R_{x}}{R_{T}}$$
![[Pasted image 20260108034304.png]]
Ratio of voltages across series resistors will be the same as the ratio of their resistances 
$$\begin{align*}
R_{T}&=6+3+1=10\ \Omega\\
V_{1}&=20\times \left( \frac{6}{10} \right)=12\ V\\
V_{2}&=20\times \left( \frac{3}{10} \right)=6\ V\\
V_{3}&=20\times \left( \frac{1}{10} \right)=2\ V\\
V_{1}:V_{2}:V_{3}&=12:6:2=6:3:1
\end{align*}$$

# Notation

<h4>Single Subscript Notation</h4>

Electrical systems are grounded for references and safety purposes
If $V_{a}$ and $V_{b}$ are measured with respect to ground then a single subscript notation can be used and the ground reference is implied ![[Pasted image 20260108034848.png]]

<h4>Double Subscript Notation</h4>
The two points denote the voltage across the resistor
Since a is first in $V_{ab}$ , point a must have higher potential for $V_{ab}$ to be +ve
The order of the subscript indicates that the potential (voltage) at point a is measured with respect to point b 
- If $V_{ab}$ is +ve then $V_{a}$ is at a higher potential than $V_{b}$
- If $V_{ab}$ is -ve then $V_{a}$ is at a lower potential than $V_{b}$

# Internal Resistance

Only considered "ideal" voltage sources

In reality all voltage sources have internal resistance that can cause unexpected results at the terminals
![[Pasted image 20260108035254.png]]

![[Pasted image 20260108035939.png]]
Applying Kirchhoff's Voltage Law with no load voltage ($V_{NL}$) on our real source:
$$\begin{align*}
E-V_{\text{int}R}-V_{NL}&=0\ V\\
(V_{\text{int}R}=IR_{\text{int}})\\
E-IR_{\text{int}}-V_{NL}&=0\ V\\
\text{Since}\ R_{T}=\infty\ \Omega, I&=0\ A\\
\therefore E&=V_{NL}
\end{align*}$$

![[Pasted image 20260108035946.png]]
Applying Kirchhoff's Voltage Law with load: 
$$\begin{align*}
E-IR_{\text{int}}-V_{L}&=0\\
\therefore V_{L}=E-I&R_{\text{int}}\\
\text{Since}\ E=V_{NL}\\
\therefore V_{L}=V_{NL}-I&R_{\text{int}}
\end{align*}$$
Normally access is only available to the terminals, i.e. $R_{\text{int}}$ is not known

Procedure for finding $R_{\text{int}}$ is:
1. Measure $V_{NL}$
2. Connect the load : measure $I$ and $V_L$
3. Solve $V_{L}=V_{NL}-IR_{\text{int}}$ for $R_{\text{int}}$

E.g.
![[Pasted image 20260108040143.png]]
Find $R_{\text{int}}$
$$\begin{align*}
E&=V_{NL}\\
V_{L}&=V_{NL}-IR_{\text{int}}\\
R_{\text{int}}&=\frac{V_{NL}-V_{L}}{I}\\
V_{L}&=IR_{L}\\
V_{L}&=0.005\times 2200 = 11\ V\\
R_{\text{int}}&=\frac{12-11}{0.005}=200\ \Omega
\end{align*}$$
